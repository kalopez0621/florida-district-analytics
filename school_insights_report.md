# District Data Review: Three Miami-Dade Middle Schools
## Assessment Insights from FAST PM3 (2025-26)

This report is a full analysis of 1,505 students across three Miami-Dade County middle schools using Florida's FAST assessment framework. It covers ELA and Math proficiency, learning gains, subgroup equity, growth trajectories, and actionable intervention targets. All calculations follow FLDOE accountability rules.

---

## How FLDOE School Grades Work

Florida middle schools are graded on 9 components, each worth up to 100 percentage points for a total of 900 possible points:

1. ELA Achievement
2. ELA Learning Gains
3. ELA Learning Gains Lowest 25%
4. Math Achievement
5. Math Learning Gains
6. Math Learning Gains Lowest 25%
7. MS Acceleration (Algebra 1 EOC + Biology EOC + Geometry EOC)
8. Science Achievement (8th graders not enrolled in Biology EOC)
9. Social Studies Achievement (7th grade Civics EOC only)

For middle schools, the school grade is determined by the total percentage points earned divided by 900: A = 64% or greater, B = 57% to 63%, C = 44% to 56%, D = 34% to 43%, F = 33% or less.

**Scope of this analysis.** This project covers 7 of the 9 components. Science Achievement and Social Studies Achievement are not included because the dataset focuses on Math, ELA, and Accelerated Math only. MS Acceleration in this dataset only includes Algebra 1 EOC. The full FLDOE MS Acceleration formula also includes Biology EOC and Geometry EOC, which are not in this dataset. The 7 components analyzed here drive the majority of a school's total points.

## Somerset Academy Bay (6128): The A School

**Strengths.** Somerset leads in every School Grades component. ELA proficiency at 88% and MS Acceleration at 87% are standout numbers. ELA learning gains (72.6%) and math learning gains (71.4%) show consistent growth across the student body. The PM1-to-PM3 ELA trajectory shows steady upward movement from 241.8 to 251.2, a gain of 9.4 scale score points on average.

**Where to look closer.** Math proficiency at 90.0% is strong, but the bubble student analysis still found 10 students within 10 points of the math proficiency cut. Targeted math intervention for this small group could push math achievement even higher.

**ELL gap.** Somerset's ELL population is small (15%), but the ELA proficiency gap between ELL (75.0%) and non-ELL (90.4%) students is 15.4 percentage points. For an A school, this gap is worth monitoring. ELL students are proficient at a rate most schools would celebrate, but the gap signals that language support could be tightened.

**Recommendation.** Somerset is performing well overall. The ELL proficiency gap (15.4 points) is the main area for growth. Strengthening language support in ELA could narrow that gap while maintaining the school's A status. The 10 math bubble students are a secondary target for quick wins.

## Lawton Chiles Middle (6161): The B School

**Strengths.** Lawton Chiles has strong math learning gains (67.1%), close to their math proficiency (59.7%). MS Acceleration at 75% is solid. ELA learning gains (62.3%) are respectable.

**The growth trajectory problem.** The PM1-to-PM3 ELA trajectory shows a concerning pattern: average scores went up from PM1 (238.3) to PM2 (242.9), then dropped at PM3 (234.0). That is a net loss of 4.3 points across the year. Students were improving mid-year, then something changed. This could be an instructional shift, attendance patterns in the spring testing window, or assessment fatigue. The PM2-to-PM3 decline needs investigation.

**ELL gap.** With 30% ELL students, the ELL/non-ELL proficiency gap is 13.2 percentage points in ELA (48.8% ELL vs 62.0% non-ELL). This gap is narrower than Somerset's in absolute terms, but the overall proficiency is much lower. Nearly half of ELL students are not proficient in ELA.

**Bubble students.** At PM2, 46 ELA students and 52 math students were within 10 points of proficiency. These students were close enough to convert with targeted support. But the outcome data tells a different story: the vast majority of these bubble students dropped at PM3 instead of crossing the line. Somerset converted roughly 80% of its bubble students. Lawton Chiles converted almost none. That gap in conversion rate is as important as the gap in proficiency.

**Recommendation.** Three priorities. First, investigate the PM2-to-PM3 score decline. Pull attendance data for the PM3 testing window, compare instructional pacing between semesters, and check whether test prep strategies shifted after PM2. Second, build a bubble student intervention protocol. The students were identified at PM2. The data existed to act on. The question is whether teachers had the list, the time, and the support structure to reach them. Third, study what Somerset does between PM2 and PM3 that maintains student momentum. Their 80% conversion rate is not an accident.

## Henry H. Filer Middle (6171): The C School

**Strengths.** Filer has the largest student body (732) and the highest ELL population (40%). Despite the C grade, their ELA LG Lowest 25% rate (55.9%) shows that the lowest-performing students are making gains. Math LG Lowest 25% is also reasonable. MS Acceleration at 65% means a majority of Algebra 1 students are passing the EOC at Level 3+.

**The core challenge.** ELA proficiency at 48.4% and math proficiency at 46.4% are below district averages. Both subjects are under 50%, meaning more students are not proficient than are. The PM1-to-PM3 ELA trajectory drops from 231.4 to 228.3, a loss of 3.0 points. Like Lawton Chiles, Filer shows a mid-year bump at PM2 (236.1) followed by a PM3 decline.

**ELL gap.** This is where the data tells the most important story. The ELL/non-ELL ELA proficiency gap at Filer is 21.0 percentage points (36.5% ELL vs 57.5% non-ELL). With 40% of the school being ELL, this gap drives the overall school grade more than any other factor. Closing this gap by even a third would move school-wide ELA proficiency above 50%.

**Bubble students.** At PM2, Filer had the largest bubble population across all three schools: 90 ELA and 134 math students within 10 points of proficiency. Like Lawton Chiles, almost none of these students crossed the line by PM3. The majority dropped. With 40% ELL population and the highest bubble count, Filer has both the greatest need and the greatest opportunity for intervention impact.

**Recommendation.** Four priorities, in order of impact. First, build an early warning system that flags bubble students at PM1, not PM2. By PM2, there are only weeks before PM3. Schools need the full second semester to intervene, not just the final stretch. Second, ELL-focused ELA instruction. The 21-point ELL gap is the single largest driver of the C grade. Integrating language development into core ELA instruction rather than pulling students out for separate ESOL would reach more students without losing instructional time. Third, assign bubble students to specific interventionists with weekly progress checks. The data shows these students were identified but not moved. That is a systems problem, not a data problem. Fourth, coordinate with Lawton Chiles on the shared PM2-to-PM3 decline. If both schools are losing ground in the spring, the root cause may be district-level (curriculum pacing, testing calendar, staffing) rather than school-level.

## Cross-School Patterns

**The PM2-to-PM3 decline.** Both Lawton Chiles and Filer show scores improving from PM1 to PM2, then declining at PM3. Somerset does not show this pattern. This could mean that lower-performing schools face spring-semester challenges like attendance drops, testing fatigue, or curriculum pacing issues that higher-performing schools have systems to manage. A district-level investigation comparing spring attendance and instructional continuity across school grades would be valuable.

**Math vs ELA.** Math proficiency is close to ELA at Somerset (90.0% vs 88.0%) and Lawton Chiles (59.7% vs 57.8%) but falls behind at Filer (46.4% vs 48.4%). Math learning gains are strong at Lawton Chiles (67.1%), which means the instruction is working and students are growing toward the proficiency line.

**ELL as the equity lever.** The ELL proficiency gap ranges from 13.2 to 21.0 percentage points across schools. At Filer, where 40% of students are ELL, this gap is the primary driver of the school grade. Any platform serving these schools needs to surface ELL performance as a first-class metric, not buried in a subgroup filter.

**Bubble student conversion is the real differentiator.** All three schools had bubble students at PM2. Somerset converted roughly 80% of them to proficient by PM3. Lawton Chiles and Filer converted almost none. Most dropped instead. The number of bubble students matters less than what the school does with that information. A school that identifies 50 bubble students and moves 40 of them will outperform a school that identifies 100 and moves none. The intervention protocol, not the data, is the bottleneck.

## What This Means for a Data Platform

If I were presenting this analysis to district administrators through a platform like Newsela's Schoolytics, these are the features that would matter most:

**Bubble student identification at PM1, not just PM2.** The data shows bubble students were identifiable at PM2 but schools failed to convert them by PM3. The platform should flag bubble students after PM1, giving schools an entire semester to intervene. After PM2, update the list and show which PM1 bubble students are still below the line. By PM3, the platform should show conversion rates so schools can evaluate whether their interventions worked.

**Bubble-to-outcome tracking.** Identifying bubble students is step one. The platform needs to close the loop: were these students assigned to an intervention? Did the intervention happen? Did their scores move? Without this tracking, the data stays in a dashboard and never reaches the classroom. The difference between Somerset's 80% conversion rate and Filer's near-zero rate is not about data access. It is about what happens after the data is seen.

**ELL performance as a default view, not a filter.** For schools like Filer where 40% of students are ELL, hiding subgroup data behind a dropdown means hiding the most important story. The platform should surface ELL gaps on the main dashboard when ELL population exceeds a threshold.

**Growth trajectory alerts.** The PM2-to-PM3 decline at two of three schools is the kind of pattern that gets missed when schools only look at final results. A platform that shows PM1-PM2-PM3 trends and flags score declines between windows would give administrators and coaches time to investigate and course-correct before the next assessment.

**Impact modeling.** Showing a principal that moving 20 bubble students to proficient changes their school grade from a C to a B is more compelling than any proficiency table. The platform should let administrators run what-if scenarios: "If we move X students, what happens to our metrics?" This analysis showed that Filer had 134 math bubble students at PM2. Even converting 30% of them would have changed the school's math achievement rate significantly.
